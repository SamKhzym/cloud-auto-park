\section{Executive summary}

% \id{Summarize the project. Identify link with your own research if applicable. If the output is meant to be published, please, clearly state it, so that the project receives appropriate attention from the instructor.}

This project aims to develop a digital twin for the automotive space which runs in the cloud and which the physical twin (one-tenth scale vehicle) communicates with wirelessly. The digital twin periodically receives vehicle kinematics data continuously from the physical twin during manual driving to update a vehicle dynamics model. This involves vehicle speed, acceleration, yaw rate, and GPS location. This allows for continuous updates to the vehicle model parameters to allow for any deviations in vehicle parameters (mass, tire pressure, etc). It will also evaluate the tolerance of the digital twin to perform well under an unreliable transmission medium (wireless).

Once the physical twin is ready to do an automated parking maneuver, which is generally very compute and memory heavy, it will transmit its information to the digital twin. This involves image and point cloud data from the onboard camera and LiDAR. The digital twin will then determine an optimal trajectory for the physical twin to follow and transmit said trajectory to the physical twin, which will then attempt to follow the provided trajectory.

The intention of the majority of the work is to go towards my Master's thesis, and I would be interested in writing a journal paper on the digital twin system design and fault-tolerant properties of the system.

All code and analysis the author has written for the purposes of this project can be found at the following GitHub link: \url{https://github.com/SamKhzym/cloud-auto-park}.

\section{Scope and requirements}

\subsection{Scope}

The scope of the project will be broken up into a physical twin (the vehicle), and the digital twin (cloud-based application). The physical twin shall be implemented in two domains, simulation and in-vehicle. 

The first domain for the physicial twin is in simulation, where a virtual vehicle model provided by creators of the F1TENTH framework with variable dynamics parameters. The virtual vehicle model shall represent the physical twin and periodically communicate its state to the digital twin while manually driving. The state of the simulated physical twin will consist of its GPS location, speed, yaw rate, acceleration, and steering wheel angle. The digital twin will periodically re-optimize the vehicle parameters on the most recently received vehicle data. Once the auto-park maneuver starts, the simulated vehicle shall send the camera and LiDAR data to the digital twin, which will find the obstacles using SLAM and determine an optimal path for the simulated vehicle to follow with the best vehicle dynamics parameters determined by the parameter optimizer. The simulation will also contain a setting to drop a percentage of incoming packets to the digital twin, to simulate the unreliability of the medium in practical applications.

The second domain for the physical twin is in-vehicle, which will use McSCert's Mini M-City one-tenth scale vehicles. The vehicle shall report the same information as the simulated physical twin, with the exception of using a localization technology such as AprilTags to determine the global position of the vehicle in the room in the absence of GPS information.

The digital twin shall run on a server which will receive the periodic information from the digital twin, and periodically solve an optimization problem to update the vehicle dynamics parameters. It shall use a timeseries database to store and retreive previous dynamics information as well. During the parking maneuver, the digital twin shall receive the LiDAR and camera data from the physical twin (either real or simulated), run a path planning algorithm to determine the trajectory to follow, then transmit the path to follow and the vehicle parameters to the physical twin for it to execute the commands to follow the trajectory with its own controller.

\subsection{Requirements}

Physical Twin Requirements:
\begin{itemize}
    \item PHYS-1: The physical twin shall be controllable by a human operator using a game controller.
    \begin{itemize}

        \item PHYS-2.1: The digital twin shall estimate the vehicle's mass, cornering stiffnesses, and yaw inertia to within 10\% accuracy.

    \item DIGI-4: The digital twin shall be able to localize the poses of obstacles in the environment from the LiDAR and camera data provided by the physical twin to within 5cm accuracy.
    \item DIGI-5: The digital twin shall be able to determine the location of the desired pose by detecting the red paper in the camera frame to within 5cm accuracy.
    \item DIGI-6: The digital twin shall be able to calculate a trajectory to move the vehicle from its initial pose to the desired final pose.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Software}

The project is broken up into seven unique software components, three of which run on the physical twin, and four of which run on the digital twin. Each of these software components shall be implemented as a ROS2 node in Python or C++.

The software components on the physical twin are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The Data Collection component will take all sensor data from the connected sensors on the vehicle as inputs and is responsible for transmitting it to the Data Management component on the digital twin.
    \item The Manual Controller will be listening to the connected joystick and actuate the drive/steering motor based on the operator commands.
    \item The Auto-Park Vehicle Controller will transmit the desired pose to the digital twin and receive the trajectory to follow and updated vehicle dynamics parameters. It will then actuate the driving and steering motors to follow the provided trajectory. 
\end{itemize}

The software components on the digital twin are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The Data Management component will receive the sensor data from the physical twin and save them to a timeseries database which can be queried by timestamps to get all data from specific time regions.
    \item The Vehicle Parameter Optimizer will take the IMU information and the GPS information from the physical twin and periodically run an optimization sweep to determine the most accurate vehicle dynamics parameters. This will be running the dynamic vehicle bicycle model, which requires the vehicle mass, yaw intertia, cornering stiffnesses, and distances from center of gravity to axles. 
    \item The SLAM + Obstacle Detection component will take the LiDAR and camera data from the physical twin and run a SLAM algorithm to localize all the obstacles in the vehicle's environment.
    \item The Auto-Park Trajectory Planner will take in the obstacle poses from the obstacle detection component and the desired final pose from the physical twin, then send them to the trajectory planner on the digital twin. When it receives the trajectory to follow and the most up-to-date vehicle dynamics parameters, it will generate the trajectory for the physical twin to follow.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Middleware}

All inter-process communication from within each hardware component shall be handled as ROS2 topics. The sensor data during the manual driving phase shall be transmitted as a UDP stream since it is not required for all packets to make it to the server. During an auto-park maneuver, the transmission protocol shall switch to TCP since packet loss is more detrimental in that environment.

\subsection{Data Collection (Physical Twin)}

The Data Collection component implementation is quite trivial and involves a ROS2 node listening to the \verb|/ego_racecar/odom|, \verb|/drive|, and \verb|/scan| topics, then sending that information to the Data Management node on the digital twin (although this communication has been dropped since all communication interfaces are virtual for the purposes of the final submission). Since it is uncommon for vehicle applications to be working with a world reference frame, as the simulation environment provides, helper functions were written to convert the global x-y coordinates to a pair of GPS coordinates which are more commonly used in vehicle applications for global localization. This was done using the Haversine equations and deriving simplified inverse Haversine equations to go from GPS to global coordinates and vice versa. Note that the origin is an arbitrary initial GPS coordinate pair $(\phi_{ref}, \lambda_{ref})$ used as a reference for all other calculations. For all following equations, $R$ represents the radius of the Earth.

Core Haversine Equation:

\begin{equation}
    d_{hav}(\phi_1, \lambda_1, \phi_2, \lambda_2) = 2R \arcsin\left(\sqrt{\sin^2\left(\frac{\phi_2 - \phi_1}{2}\right) + \cos(\phi_1)\cos(\phi_2)\sin^2\left(\frac{\lambda_2 - \lambda_1}{2}\right)}\right)
\end{equation}

Signed Haversine Equations (Conversion from GPS to Cartesian Coordinates):

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Delta m_{lat} &= \operatorname{sgn}(\phi_{new} - \phi_{ref}) \cdot d_{hav}(\phi_{ref}, \lambda_{ref}, \phi_{new}, \lambda_{ref}) \\
\Delta m_{long} &= \operatorname{sgn}(\lambda_{new} - \lambda_{ref}) \cdot d_{hav}(\phi_{ref}, \lambda_{ref}, \phi_{ref}, \lambda_{new}) \\
x_{ego} &= \Delta m_{long} \sin(\theta) + \Delta m_{lat} \cos(\theta) \\
y_{ego} &= -(\Delta m_{long} \cos(\theta)) + \Delta m_{lat} \sin(\theta) \\
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Inverse Haversine Equations (conversion from Cartesian to GPS Coordinates):
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\phi &= \frac{x_{ego}}{R} + \phi_{ref}\\
\lambda &= 2 \arcsin \left( \frac{\sin\left(\frac{-y_{ego}}{2R}\right)}{\cos(\phi_{ref})} \right) + \lambda_{ref}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Vehicle Parameter Optimizer (Digital Twin)}

The Vehicle Parameter Optimizer component currently is not fully set up as a ROS2 node, but the core implementation exists in a Jupyter notebook called \verb|process_driving_data.ipynb|. The core function of this component, and one of the major premises of including a cloud-based digital twin to this particular application, is that it allows the controller to be adaptable to changes in the system (mass or tire changes, etc.) long after the original software calibration set had been deployed.

The mathematical model used to simulate vehicle plant model dynamics for many model-based controllers is called the dynamic vehicle bicycle model, which treats each axle of the vehicle as an individual wheel. For accurate system modeling, this model relies on six vehicle parameters. These are the distances from the front and rear axles to the center of gravity ($l_f$, $l_r$), the front and rear tire cornering stiffnesses ($C_f$, $C_r$), the mass ($m$), and the moment of inertia about the vertical axis ($I_z$). The state update equation is shown in Equation \ref{eq:vbm}, with the lateral velocity ($v_y$) and yaw rate ($\omega$) being the state variables, and the vehicle's road wheel angle ($\delta$) as the input variable. The vehicle speed is assumed to be known from a different source and incorporated in the $A$ matrix as $v_x$. This is integrated forward by using the Forward Euler method, shown in equation \ref{eq:forward_euler}.

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:vbm}
\underbrace{
\begin{bmatrix} \dot{v_y} \\ \dot{\omega} \end{bmatrix}
}_\mathbf{\dot{x}} =
\underbrace{
\left[ \begin{smallmatrix}
-\frac{C_f + C_r}{m v_x} & \frac{l_r C_r - l_f C_f}{m v_x} - v_x \\
\frac{l_r C_r - l_f C_f}{I_z v_x} & -\frac{l_f^2 C_f + l_r^2 C_r}{I_z v_x}
\end{smallmatrix} \right]}_A
\underbrace{
\begin{bmatrix} v_y \\ \omega \end{bmatrix}}_\mathbf{x} +
\underbrace{
\begin{bmatrix} \frac{C_f}{m} \\ \frac{l_f C_f}{I_z} \end{bmatrix}
}_B
\delta
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:forward_euler}
\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{\dot{x}}_k \cdot \Delta t + \mathbf{x}_k
\end{equation}

The global vehicle pose can also be tracked using the state variables from the bicycle model and integrated using the Forward Euler method, shown in Equation \ref{eq:pose_update}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pose_update}
\begin{bmatrix} 
\dot{x} \\ \dot{y} \\ \dot{\psi}
\end{bmatrix} = 
\begin{bmatrix} 
\cos\psi & -\sin\psi & 0\\ 
\sin\psi & \cos\psi & 0\\
0 & 0 & 1\\
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} 
v_x \\ v_y \\ \omega
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

While the axle locations are unlikely to ever change, the mass ditribution and tire dynamics could change if the driver decides to replace the tires, or if they are carrying something heavy that throws off the original distribution estimate. While these parameters may not deviate much from the original parameter set immediately, it is likely that the lifetime of a normal vehicle will cause enough parameter drift due to maintenance and repair will cause the accuracy of these parameters to drift, thus degrading model-based controller performance.

An online estimator for these parameters was created by taking a manual driving log over a sufficiently long period (1-2 minutes), and optimizing the $C_f, C_r, m, I_z$ parameters to achieve the closest performance between predicted and measured variables over the yaw rate and vehicle path of the driving data. Two distinct root mean square error (RMSE) terms are used as metrics. The $\lambda_\omega$ and $\lambda_{path}$ hyperparameters control which term is more influential in the cost function.

\begin{equation}
\label{rmse_omega}
RMSE_{\omega} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N (\omega_{i} - \hat{\omega_{i}})^2}\\
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{rmse_path}
RMSE_{path} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N (x_{i} - \hat{x_{i}})^2 + (y_{i} - \hat{y_{i}})^2}\\
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{cornering_stiffness_cost_function}
C_f^{\star}, C_r^{\star}, m^\star, I_z^\star = \underset{C_f, C_r, m, I_z}{\mathrm{argmin}} (\lambda_{\omega} \cdot RMSE_{\omega} + \lambda_{path} \cdot RMSE_{path})\\
\end{equation}

This optimization was only performed once for the purposes of this project, but a future version of this project should have it running periodically on more recent driving data to consistently update the parameters.

\subsection{Auto-Park Trajectory Planner (Digital Twin)}

The Auto-Park Trajectory Planner component receives the obstacle map from the obstacle detection component. In its current iteration, it calculates the desired pose of the parking vehicle to be in-between two existing parked vehicles, although there should be additional work to this component to determine the best desired pose given the free space of the environment. It then uses the vehicle's immediate state as the reference pose, and constructs a cubic spline with control points placed in geometrically pre-determined locations to try and minimize sharp turns during the parking maneuver. An example of the control point selection and path generation is shown in Figure \ref{fig:traj_gen}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{img/traj_gen.png}
  \caption{Generated Cubic Spline Trajectory with Control Points}
  \label{fig:traj_gen}

The trajectory generation is done assuming a constant speed, and with the final trajectory quantized such that each control point corresponds to the desired vehicle pose at each controller step. It is also currently optimized for perpendicular parking and does not work well on angled or parallel parking, which will be the exploration of future work as well. The implementation uses the \verb|splprep| and \verb|splev| implementations from \verb|scipy|, which is convenient as it also allows to return the curvature of the path at every point.

This component is structured as a ROS2 service (\verb|cloud_auto_park_srv.py|), which the client (physical twin) can call by providing the desired final vehicle pose (to be removed in a future version and replaced with outputs of the obstacle detector), the desired vehicle speed, and the controller sample rate. Once the trajectory generation is complete, it is packed as a \verb|Trajectory| custom message as a list of points with an x-position, y-position, and curvature, and sent along with the optimal vehicle bicycle parameters back to the physical twin, implemented as the \verb|BicycleModelParams| message.

\subsection{Auto-Park Vehicle Controller (Physical Twin)}

The Auto-Park Vehicle Controller receives the optimal vehicle bicycle model parameters and the desired trajectory from the digital twin, and uses a model-predictive controller (MPC) to determine the control actions for the physical twin. This involves solving an online optimization problem over a control horizon $N_p$ to determine the optimal series of control actions $\delta_{1}$ to $\delta_{N_p}$ with respect to a cost function $J(\delta_1, \dots, \delta_{N_p})$. Regularization was also added to prevent the optimizer from saturating to the input bounds in more complex environments. The first control input $\delta_1$ is then used as the command. The cost function used for this controller is purely position-based, taking the RMSE of vehicle path as the optimzation objective, as shown in Equation \ref{eq:mpc_cost_func}. 

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mpc_cost_func}
\begin{aligned}
\delta_1^{\star}, \dots, \delta_{N_p}^{\star} &= \underset{\delta_1, \dots, \delta_{N_p}}{\mathrm{argmin}} (RMSE_{path} + \lambda \cdot || \mathbf{\delta} ||_2)\\
&\text{s.t.} |\delta_i| \leq \delta_{\max} 
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The \verb|scipy.optimize.minimize| function was used with the SLSQP option for least squares optimization, allowing for relatively fast convergence for an online optimizer. The control horizon length $N_p$ is a hyperparameter which was tuned to allow for quick convergence time while maintaining sufficient performance. The implementation of this component is in \verb|mpc.py| and the ROS2 client which calls the trajectory planner service is implemented in \verb|parking_controller.py|.
